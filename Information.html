Learn

Know the signs. Act on them.

Early detection saves lives — but only if people know what to look for and where to go. Here's a plain-language starting point on cancer risk, warning signs, and screening.

This page is for general awareness only and isn't medical advice. Always talk to a doctor about your personal risk and screening schedule.
Why Early Detection Matters

Timing changes outcomes more than almost anything else.

Cancer caught in its earliest stages, before it has spread, is generally far easier to treat and far more survivable. The challenge is that early stages often come with few or no symptoms — which is exactly why awareness and routine screening matter so much.

90%
5-year survival rate when cancer is caught early
— American Cancer Society
1in 2
Men and women who will be diagnosed with cancer in their lifetime
— American Cancer Society
Warning Signs

Changes worth paying attention to.

None of these mean cancer on their own — but any of them, especially if new or persistent, are worth mentioning to a doctor.

01

Unexplained lumps or swelling

Any new lump, thickening, or swelling that doesn't go away, especially if it's growing or firm to the touch.

Read more
02

Unexplained weight loss

Losing a noticeable amount of weight without changes to diet or activity level.

Read more
03

Persistent fatigue

Tiredness that doesn't improve with rest and interferes with daily activities over weeks.

Read more
04

Changes in skin

New moles, or existing moles that change in size, shape, or color; sores that don't heal.

Read more
05

Persistent pain

Pain that doesn't have a clear cause and doesn't go away after a few weeks.

Read more
06

Changes in bowel or bladder habits

Ongoing changes in frequency, consistency, or noticeable blood.

Screening Basics

Routine checks catch what symptoms can't.

Screening guidelines vary by age, sex, and family history — talk to a doctor about what applies to you. These are some of the most common starting points.

01

Breast cancer

Mammograms are generally recommended starting around age 40–45, or earlier with family history.

Read more
02

Colorectal cancer

Screening, such as colonoscopy, is generally recommended starting around age 45.

Read more
03

Cervical cancer

Pap tests are generally recommended starting around age 21, on a regular schedule.

Read more
04

Skin cancer

Annual skin checks, plus self-exams for new or changing moles, at any age.

Read more
05

Lung cancer

Low-dose CT screening may be recommended for long-term smokers starting around age 50.

Read more
06

Prostate cancer

PSA testing is often discussed starting around age 50, or earlier with family history.

Read more
